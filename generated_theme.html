The Gentle Art of Pruning Roses in Late Autumn

By Eleanor Vance October 14, 2023

There is a specific rhythm to the garden as the days grow shorter. The frantic growth of July settles into the golden hues of October, and the roses, tired from their display, ask for a gentle hand.

Many gardeners approach pruning with a sense of dread, fearing they might cut too much or in the wrong place. But for the Sunday gardener, pruning is not a chore but a conversation with the plant. It is about airflow, light, and preparing for the winter dormancy.

Why Wait for Dormancy?

Patience is the gardener's most valuable tool. Cutting back your roses while they are still pushing energy into blooms can stimulate new growth that will freeze when the first frost hits. Wait until the leaves have yellowed or fallen. This signals that the sap has returned to the roots.

The Three Cuts You Need to Know

We keep things simple here. You don't need a degree in horticulture. Focus on the three D's: Dead, Damaged, and Diseased. Removing these canes first opens up the structure of the plant and makes the remaining decisions much easier.

  • • Dead Wood: Brown, brittle, and hollow. Cut it back to the base.
  • • Crossing Branches: Canes rubbing against each other create wounds. Remove the weaker of the two.
  • • Thin Growth: Anything thinner than a pencil won't support a good bloom next year.

Once you have cleared the clutter, step back. The goal is an open vase shape. This allows sunlight to reach the center of the plant come spring, reducing the risk of fungal diseases. It’s not just about aesthetics; it’s about health.
